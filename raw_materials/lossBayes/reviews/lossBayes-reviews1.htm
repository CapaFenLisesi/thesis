Detailed Comments This paper addresses an important problem: approximating the optimal action in a Bayesian decision-theoretic framework. Ideally the problem can be split into calculating the posterior distribution and taking the action that minimises the expected loss, but this is no longer safe if the solution has to be approximated.

The 'approximation framework' proposed in this paper is elegant and natural, albeit in hindsight somewhat obvious. It is presented clearly and motivated well enough. The power plant example is very nice.

I'm not so sure what to think of the proposed algorithm for actually calculating the optimal approximate q. In equation (6), the optimisation problem is defined. Then a utility function is defined as M minus the loss, where M is an arbitrary fixed constant. Maximising the utility is equivalent to minimising the loss, so this far, the optimisation problem remains equivalent. The authors then propose an algorithm for approximating the solution to the problem that depends on M - whereas the optimal solution does not depend on M. Thus, although the algorithm has been endowed with a parameter that we can play around with, it seems to me that the effect of this parameter can be anything: we are given not much intuition as to its relationship to the solution we're interested in. I'm not particularly convinced by "M [...] allows us to interpolate between the standard KL-approach and the loss-EM".

Another problem with introducing the fixed constant M is that it only applies to bounded loss functions - which precludes logarithmic loss and square loss, which are of paramount importance.

Section 4 I found much too terse- I'm sure it's possible for highly motivated readers to figure out exactly what's going on here, but since it's just about an example to gain some insight into the proposed algorithm, the presentation needs to be simplified drastically here. In general, the writing in Sections 4 and 5 was substantially worse than in the other sections (different author?) Please make Section 4 easier to read by going into less detail, and clean up Section 5.

I was pleased that the experimental results seem to paint a balanced picture: there is no attempt to hide the fact that EP usually performs much better that the other considered approaches. At the same time, the results do suggest that further improvements over standard approaches should be possible, maybe using methods along the lines proposed in this paper.


Here is a list of minor issues.


- Words like section, table, equation, etc. should be capitalized if they're followed by a number (proper nouns).
- It's either Equation 3 or simply (3), but not equation (3).
- One hypothesis, two hypotheses.

Section 2.2
-----------
- "-- minimizes d_{KL}(q||p_D)" -> "(which minimizes ...)", same for variational inference. Also, you never define p_D to be the posterior.
- "It therefore makes sense to say that an approximate distribution q is [...] to the posterior risk of the p_D-optimal decision". This implication is the wrong way around: if two distributions are close, then the risks of their optimal decisions must be close, but not necessarily the other way around.
- "[...] measures the cost of coding [...]" -> "[...] measures the expected cost of coding [...]"
- "shut-down the power plant" -> "shut the power plant down"
- "If the temperature [...], keeping it running will cause a nuclear meltdown [...]" -> "A temperature above the critical level T_crit will cause a meltdown [...]"
- "q_3 is an example of q_opt in this setting": ugly, rephrase

Section 3
---------
- "which minimizes [...]" -> "that minimizes"

Section 3.2
-----------
- "M > > L": use the correct symbol for much larger than
- "For large M\to\infty, the linearized and the original algorithms become almost equivalent": either use "For large M" or "For M\to\infty", both is a bit strange

Section 4
---------
- "We can obtain in this case X": wrong word order, happens repeatedly. Should be "In this case we can obtain X" or "we can obtain X in this case". Other examples: "We cannot X unfortunately as Y" (should be: "Unfortunately, we cannot X as Y", or "We cannot X, as unfortunately Y"). "We can get nevertheless" -> "We can nevertheless get", and so on.

Section 4.1
-----------
- "coming iid from [...]": you can't "come iid from" something
- "where we have defined" -> "where" (you haven't defined it yet)
- "in term of" -> "in terms of" (also occurs repeatedly)

Section 4.2
-----------
- "Var": it seems to be typeset as $Var$, which interprets it as a product of V, a and r. This looks very ugly. Operators should not be in italics and typeset in text mode, not math mode. For example \newcommand{\Var}{\textnormal{Var}}
- "won't", "doesn't" -> "will not", "does not"

Section 5
---------
- "R_{p_D}(-h_{p_D}) is the posterior risk [...]": there should not be a minus sign there.
- "predicts the opposite than the posterior would" -> "predicts the opposite of the posterior"
- "values between 0(posterior-optimal) and 1(maximum risk)": there should be spaces between the numbers and the parentheses
- "[...] loss-EM provided improvement": article missing
- "direct Laplace approximation to the posterior" -> "... of the posterior"
- "EP is also known to be [...] case": this sentence is broken

Detailed Comments This paper provides an interesting and valuable perspective on Bayesian analyses involving approximate inference. It is a real written paper and I don't have serious issues with the approach. However, it would be nice to also see a real data application where the proposed methodology actually makes a substantive difference over the usual strategy of minimizing the q-risk. It would also be great to see an evaluation of how good bound in (10) is in cases where it is not tight, and the M >> L approximation in section 3.2 would be easier to accept if we could get more idea of the values that M takes on in real data scenarios.
